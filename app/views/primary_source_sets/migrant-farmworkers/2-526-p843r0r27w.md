## Introduction

*Thirst in the Garden* is a 30-minute documentary examining the lack of access to clean drinking water in the Mexican-American colonias of Texas’s Lower Rio Grande Valley during the 1970s. Set in one of the most agriculturally productive regions in the world, the film exposes the stark inequality faced by the farmworker communities whose labor sustained the Valley’s prosperity while many families themselves lacked safe water and basic sanitation. Residents relied on polluted irrigation canals, contaminated wells, or hauled water from distant sources, resulting in widespread disease and dangerous living conditions.

Through interviews, historical context, and on-the-ground reporting, the documentary explores how poverty, labor exploitation, rapid agricultural and economic development, and political neglect combined to create a public health crisis that remained largely invisible to the broader nation. The film highlights the irony of a region rich in water for crops but unable—or unwilling—to provide clean water for the people who worked the land, raising urgent questions about environmental justice, public health, and human rights.
