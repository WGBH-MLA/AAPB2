## Introduction

The famous Stonewall Uprising—in which LGBTQ+ people loudly and aggressively protested police harassment—is often credited as the founding event of the gay liberation movement. While LGBTQ+ rights movements predated Stonewall, efforts to promote visibility, dignity, and equality picked up steam in the 1960s and 1970s. Two prominent figures in this struggle share their views in this excerpt from a 1974 episode of The Advocates, a public affairs show on WGBH that featured a stylized debate format with advocates, witnesses, questioning, and cross-examination. The first speaker is Frank Kameny, a gay rights activist and co-founder of the Mattachine Society (arguably the first prominent organization to lobby for the rights of gay people). Kameny’s first witness is Elaine Noble, a member of the lesbian rights organization, Daughters of Bilitis. (Noble also became the first openly gay member of a state legislature in 1975, a year after this clip aired.) Both make the affirmative case for marriage equality at a time when LGBTQ+ rights organizations were first achieving national attention.
